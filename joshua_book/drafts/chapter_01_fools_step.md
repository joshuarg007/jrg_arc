# Chapter 1: The Fool's Step

---

## I.

*The Fool stands at the edge of a cliff, looking up at the sky.*

*Everyone thinks he's about to fall.*

*He knows he's about to fly.*

*The difference is faith. Or stupidity. Or the desperate hope that there's something waiting in the void besides the void itself.*

*Four Fools. One cliff. One step.*

*Here we go.*

---

## II.

**THE MAGICIAN**

The clock on the pawnshop wall said 3:47 when he walked in.

He didn't notice. He was too busy cataloging exits—front door, back door through the employee area, window in the bathroom if he got desperate. Old habit. The projects had trained it into him before his grandfather refined it into discipline.

"Help you?" The owner was a large man with a gray beard and eyes that didn't match his folksy appearance. Too sharp. Too knowing.

"Ring." The Magician pulled it from his pocket. Gold band, small diamond, worn thin by decades of wear. His grandmother's. The last valuable thing he owned. "How much?"

The owner examined it. "Sentimental value?"

"Does it matter?"

"Changes what I offer. People sell their memories, they should get fair price." The owner looked up. "Eighty dollars."

"It's worth five times that."

"It's worth what someone will pay. Right now, standing in my shop at 3:47 on a Tuesday afternoon, that's eighty dollars."

The Magician took it.

The transaction was quick. Cash in hand, ring in the drawer, no receipt offered or asked for. He turned to leave.

"Word of advice," the owner said.

The Magician stopped.

"Time's been wrong lately. You notice that?"

"I've noticed."

"Getting worse. The cracks are spreading. Used to be you'd lose a few minutes here and there. Now it's hours. Days, sometimes. The veil's tearing, boy. Has been for months. Whatever you're running from, whatever you're running toward—" The owner's eyes held something that wasn't quite human. "Better run faster."

The Magician walked out.

It said 3:47 on the clock when he left. It said 3:47 when he reached his car and found an hour of ice on the windshield. It said 3:47 when the boy in the passenger seat—his nephew, Marcus, orphaned by the needle—looked at him with those too-old eyes.

"Time's wrong again," Marcus said.

"I know."

"Worse than last week."

The Magician started scraping the windshield with his credit card. Ice that shouldn't exist, from an hour that hadn't passed, in a world that was forgetting how to keep time.

"We should go," Marcus said. "Before it gets worse."

He turned the key. The engine caught. On the radio, a song he'd heard yesterday played for the third time this hour.

The Fool stepped off the cliff.

---

## III.

**THE PRIESTESS**

She hadn't slept in four days.

The maps covered her apartment walls—photographs, strings, pushpins in a pattern that would look like madness to anyone else. To her, it was the city's skeleton laid bare. The fractures. The fault lines. The places where reality was coming apart at the seams.

She'd been able to see them since she was twelve. The cracks that nobody else noticed. The wrongness bleeding through in colors that had no names.

Her mother had called it imagination. Her teachers had called it daydreaming. Her first psychiatrist had called it schizophrenia and prescribed pills that made the world go gray and flat and dead.

She'd stopped taking them at nineteen. The colors came back. The cracks came back. And slowly, carefully, she'd started mapping them.

The main fracture ran from the Brown Palace downtown—through the lobby, she'd discovered, through a specific room on the third floor where Andrew Carnegie had once slept and where now time moved like molasses—across Colfax Avenue, through a stretch of road where she'd lost twenty minutes just walking, down into Five Points, where the old buildings leaned away from something they could feel but not see.

Tonight it was worse.

She'd checked the crack near Colfax at 6 PM. It had been the width of a hair, visible only when she unfocused her eyes just right. Now, six hours later, it was the width of a finger. Still invisible to anyone who couldn't see what she saw. But growing.

Something was pushing from the other side.

She picked up her camera. Loaded fresh film. The photos never showed the cracks—whatever light they reflected wasn't light that film could catch—but they helped her remember. Triangulate. Track changes over time.

She headed for the door.

Stopped.

Turned back to the map.

The strings were moving.

Not physically. But in her sight—in that other way of seeing—the connections were shifting. Realigning. The fractures across the city were pulling toward four points. Four locations she hadn't identified before. Four places where something was about to happen.

She grabbed a pen. Started marking.

Point one: a pawnshop on Colfax. Point two: an apartment building on Downing Street. Point three: somewhere in the projects off Federal. Point four—

Point four was where she was standing.

The crack in the ceiling opened.

Not metaphorically. Not in her special sight. It opened. Right above her. Right through the plaster and wood and the floor of the apartment above. It opened like a mouth, and through it poured light that wasn't light, and time that had been lost started pouring through like water through a broken dam.

The Priestess threw herself sideways.

The flood missed her. Hit her maps instead. The photographs curled and aged and turned to dust. The strings rotted in seconds. The pushpins rusted where they stood.

She scrambled for the door.

The crack followed her. Racing across the ceiling. Reaching for her like a living thing.

She made it to the hallway. The crack stopped at the threshold—something about the space outside her apartment, something about the boundary between private and public, held it back.

She stood there, breathing hard, watching her apartment age decades in seconds.

Her maps. Her photographs. A year of work, disintegrating.

But she was still standing. Still seeing. And now she knew something she hadn't known before.

The four points. The four places. Something was converging.

And she was part of it.

## IV.

**THE EMPEROR**

The sickness came in waves.

He'd learned the rhythm of it. Most days, the wrongness inside him was dormant—a cold weight in his chest, a shadow at the edge of his vision, nothing more. He could work his shift at the warehouse. He could talk to his coworkers. He could pretend.

But when the waves came, pretending wasn't an option.

He gripped the edge of the bathroom sink and watched his hands shake. Behind him, in the mirror, his reflection didn't move.

"I see you," he said to it.

The reflection smiled.

*We see you too.*

"Get back."

*We are back. We're always back. We're inside you. Behind your eyes. Between your ribs. We are the thing your family was made to contain. We are the pressure. We are the weight. We are—*

"BACK."

The reflection flinched. Retreated. Became just a reflection again—tired eyes, gray-streaked hair, a face that was aging too fast.

He'd bought himself another day. Maybe two.

His family had held the line for generations. Vessels. Containers. Human prisons for something that shouldn't exist. His father had died at forty-two—heart gave out, they said, but the truth was the containment had cracked and the wrongness had flooded his organs before he could repair the breach.

His grandfather had lasted until fifty-six. His great-grandfather, fifty-one.

The Emperor was thirty-four. He could feel the walls of his containment getting thinner every year.

He splashed water on his face. Walked back out to his apartment. Small place. Sparse. Nothing that couldn't be abandoned in minutes if the wrongness broke through and he had to run.

He'd been running all his life.

On the table, a letter. From someone who shouldn't know where he lived. He'd opened it an hour ago, read it three times, and still didn't know what to make of it.

*The convergence is coming. The four who were broken will become one again. You are not alone in what you carry. Find the others. Together, you might survive.*

*The Priestess is mapping the fractures. The Magician is awakening his will. The Empress is being drained.*

*The seal is failing. The membrane is returning. You have a choice—instrument or obstacle.*

*Choose wisely.*

No signature. No return address. Just those words, in handwriting that seemed to shift when he looked at it too long.

He'd been searching for others like him all his life. Others who carried the wrongness. Others who understood what it meant to be a prison.

Now someone was telling him they existed. That there were four of them. That they were supposed to come together.

And all he could think was: *this is how they break us*.

This is how the thing inside finally gets free.

---

## V.

**THE EMPRESS**

She woke up in his arms and couldn't remember falling asleep.

This kept happening. Lost hours. Whole evenings that felt like minutes. She'd be cooking dinner, and then she'd be in bed, and the food would be cold on the counter, and he'd be holding her like nothing was wrong.

"You drifted off," he said. Always the same words. The same gentle smile.

"I've been so tired lately."

"You work too hard. Let me take care of you."

She pressed against him. Felt the tension in her shoulders ease. When she was with him, everything else faded. The exhaustion that never lifted. The nagging sense that she used to be more than this. The dreams she couldn't quite remember about light and creation and—

"Come back to me," he said.

The thought slipped away.

She loved him. She was sure she loved him. He'd found her three years ago, when she was at her lowest—drifting, purposeless, that old hollowness eating at her from the inside. He'd seen something in her that she couldn't see in herself. He'd told her she was special.

Nobody had ever told her that before.

*That's not true*, something whispered. *Someone did. A long time ago. A boy who used to walk you home.*

"What are you thinking about?" he asked.

"Nothing." She buried her face in his chest. "Just how lucky I am."

"I'm the lucky one." His arms tightened around her. "I found you. I get to keep you. Forever."

The word should have been romantic. Instead, it landed like a stone in her stomach.

*Forever.*

Something was wrong. She could feel it. Had felt it for months now—a distance between who she was and who she was supposed to be. A gap that was growing wider.

*He's taking something from you*, the whisper said. *You know he is. You just won't look.*

She pulled back. Looked at his face. The same beautiful face. The same warm eyes.

But there was something behind them. Something she'd been refusing to see.

"I need to get up," she said.

"Stay." His voice was different now. Sharper. "You need rest."

"I'm not tired."

"You're always tired." He smiled, but it didn't reach his eyes. "That's why you need me. To take care of you. To make sure you're okay."

She felt it then—the pull. The manufactured desire to stay, to comply, to sink back into his arms and forget she'd ever felt anything was wrong.

She knew she should fight it.

She couldn't remember how.

"Okay," she heard herself say. "I'll stay."

She closed her eyes. Sank back into him. The whisper went quiet.

In the corner of the room, barely visible, a crack ran through the wall. It hadn't been there yesterday. It was the width of a hair.

By morning, it would be the width of a finger.

## VI.

*Four fractures. One moment.*

*The pawnshop owner looked at the door long after the Magician left. The clock on his wall still said 3:47. It would say 3:47 for another sixteen hours.*

*"Here we go," he said to no one.*

*The Priestess stood in her hallway, watching her apartment die of old age, knowing that the map had rearranged itself, that four points had appeared, that she was one of them.*

*The Emperor sat in his sparse apartment, holding a letter that shouldn't exist, feeling the wrongness inside him stir with something that might have been hope or might have been hunger.*

*The Empress lay in the arms of the thing that was eating her, dreaming of a boy who used to walk her home, dreaming of light in her palm, dreaming of what she used to be before she forgot how to be anything at all.*

*And somewhere in the space between worlds—in the wounded veil, in the failing seal, in the cracks that were spreading through the fabric of everything—something vast shifted its attention.*

*The four were moving toward each other.*

*The convergence was coming.*

*The Fool had stepped off the cliff.*

*Now came the fall.*

---

## VII.

**THREE DAYS LATER**

The Magician found the Priestess by accident.

He was walking Colfax at 2 AM, following the feeling of wrongness that had been building in his chest since the pawnshop. Marcus was safe at the apartment—relatively safe, safer than walking these streets at night—and he needed to move, needed to understand, needed to find the source of the pressure that was making his teeth ache and his vision blur.

She was standing in the middle of the street, camera around her neck, staring at something he couldn't see.

"You're in the middle of traffic," he said.

"There isn't any traffic. There hasn't been any traffic on this stretch for three hours. Time's wrong here." She didn't look at him. "You can see it, can't you. The cracks."

"I can feel them."

"That's more than most people." She finally turned. Her eyes were red—no sleep, he recognized the look—and her hands were shaking. "You're one of them. The four. I've been waiting for you."

"How do you know what I am?"

"I see things. Patterns. Fractures." She gestured at the empty street. "Right now I can see four lines of force converging on this exact point. You're one of them. I'm another. The third is in an apartment building on Downing Street. The fourth—" She frowned. "The fourth keeps moving. Hiding. Scared of something."

"Do you know what's happening? Why the time is wrong, why the cracks are spreading?"

"The seal is failing. The veil is trying to become what it was supposed to be—a membrane, not a wall. The wrongness leaking through isn't wrong, it's just different, it's just—" She stopped. Took a breath. "I went to the tower off Champa Street. Where the order operates. They told me things. I don't know if they were true."

"What order?"

"They call themselves the Witnesses. They serve what's behind the veil. They claim they designed the prophecy—the four who are one, the convergence. They say we're not heroes. We're weapons."

The Magician felt the wrongness in his chest pulse.

"Weapons for what?"

"To break the seal completely. To let everything through at once." She hugged herself. "My apartment aged to dust. I've been on the street for three days. I don't know what's true anymore. But I know we're connected. And I know we need to find the others before the Witnesses force us together on their terms."

"The woman on Downing Street. Who is she?"

"I don't know. But something's feeding on her. Something... someone. There's a presence wrapped around her that's eating her potential. I can see the futures draining out of her like blood from a wound."

The Magician thought about his brother. About the last time he'd seen him—beautiful, smiling, lying with a saint's sincerity. About the thing that had always been wrong with him, the hunger that had never had a name.

"I might know who it is," he said. "Might know what it is."

"Then we need to help her. Before there's nothing left to save."

The Magician looked down the street. Empty. Silent. Time holding its breath.

"Together?"

The Priestess nodded.

"Together."

## VIII.

*The Fool. Card Zero.*

*He stands at the edge of everything, looking up at the sky, a small dog barking at his heels. The cliff drops away behind him. The sun is bright. His face is turned upward, away from the fall.*

*Everyone tells the story wrong. They say he's about to fall. They say he doesn't see the danger. They say he's naive, foolish, stepping into disaster.*

*But look at his face.*

*He's smiling.*

*Not because he doesn't see the cliff. Because he sees something beyond it. Something no one else can see. Something that makes the fall worthwhile.*

*Four Fools. One cliff. One step.*

*They fell.*

*And they discovered, on the way down—*

*There was no ground.*

*There never had been.*
