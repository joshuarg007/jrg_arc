# Opening Draft

---

## The Tower(s)

*Four fractures. One moment.*

---

### I.

The clock on the pawnshop wall said 3:47 when he walked in. It said 3:47 when he walked out eleven minutes later with eighty dollars for his grandmother's ring. It still said 3:47 when he reached his car and found an hour of ice on the windshield.

The boy in the passenger seat—couldn't have been older than nine—watched him scrape with a credit card. "Time's wrong again," the boy said.

"I know."

"Worse than last week."

He didn't answer. He'd learned not to count the lost hours. You drive yourself crazy trying to track what slips. The ring had been his last thing of value. The money wouldn't last a week. And the wrongness in the air, that pressure behind his eyes—it was getting stronger.

Somewhere in the city, something was cracking.

He could feel it. The way his grandfather had taught him to feel it. Before the old man's heart gave out. Before the dreams started, then stopped. Before he'd been left with training and no teacher, a tradition and no lineage.

The boy was still watching him. Eyes too old for his face.

"We should go," the boy said. "Before it gets worse."

He turned the key. The engine caught. On the radio, a song he'd heard yesterday played for the third time this hour.

He drove.

---

### II.

She hadn't slept in four days.

Not because she couldn't. Because every time she closed her eyes, she saw it—the crack running through the ceiling of her apartment, except it wasn't in the ceiling, it was behind the ceiling, behind the world, and it was spreading.

She'd traced it across the city with maps and string and photographs taken at odd hours. The fracture ran from somewhere near the Brown Palace, through a stretch of Colfax she couldn't walk without losing minutes, down into Five Points where the buildings themselves seemed to lean away from something.

It was getting wider. She could measure it now in the thickness of the distortion at its edges. Yesterday: like looking through old glass. Today: like looking through water.

Tomorrow?

She didn't want to think about tomorrow.

The books said the veil between worlds was thin in certain places. Natural weak points. Acceptable. Stable. But this wasn't thin—this was torn. This was bleeding. And nobody else seemed to notice.

Or maybe they noticed and had given up.

She hadn't given up. She wouldn't.

Somewhere in the back of her mind, underneath the exhaustion and the fear, a stubborn hope refused to die. It whispered: *this can be fixed. This can be healed. You just have to understand it.*

She picked up her camera. Loaded fresh film. Headed out into a night that was already three hours longer than the clock said it should be.

---

### III.

The sickness came in waves.

He'd learned the rhythm of it. Most days, the wrongness inside him was dormant—a cold weight in his chest, a shadow at the edge of his vision, nothing more. He could work. He could talk to people. He could pretend.

But when the waves came, pretending wasn't an option.

He gripped the edge of the sink and watched his hands shake. In the mirror, something flickered behind his reflection. He didn't look directly at it. He'd learned not to look.

*Hold the line*, he told himself. *Just hold the line.*

His family had held the line for generations. Vessels. Containers. Human prisons for something that shouldn't exist. His father. His grandfather. His great-grandfather. All the way back to someone who'd made a choice—or had a choice made for them—and condemned a bloodline to carry poison.

He didn't know if any of them had ever felt it winning. If they'd stood in bathrooms at 2 AM, feeling the walls of their containment crack. If they'd watched the shadow behind their reflection grow sharper each week.

The wave passed. The shaking stopped. The thing in his chest settled back into dormancy.

He'd bought himself another day. Maybe two.

He looked at the mirror again. Just his face now. Tired. Aging faster than the calendar said he should. But his.

For now.

---

### IV.

She woke up in his arms and couldn't remember falling asleep.

This kept happening. Lost hours. Whole evenings that felt like minutes. She'd be cooking dinner, and then she'd be in bed, and the food would be cold on the counter, and he'd be holding her like nothing was wrong.

"You drifted off," he said, when she asked. Always the same words, the same gentle smile. "You've been working so hard. You needed rest."

She had been tired. She was always tired now. A bone-deep exhaustion that sleep didn't touch. Like something was draining her. Like she was a battery being slowly emptied.

*That's crazy*, she told herself. *You're just stressed. Just overworked. Just—*

"Come back to me," he said, and pulled her closer, and the thought slipped away like water through fingers.

She loved him. She was sure she loved him. When she was with him, everything else faded—the exhaustion, the nagging wrongness, the sense that she used to be more than this. He made it quiet. He made it safe.

She pressed her face into his chest and breathed him in.

Somewhere far away, in a part of her mind she couldn't reach, something screamed.

---

### V.

*Denver. November. The year the clocks stopped keeping honest time.*

*Four people who didn't know each other. Four fractures in the same moment. The veil tearing in four places at once.*

*Something vast shifted in its sleep.*

*Something old began to pay attention.*

*And on Colfax Avenue, in a pawnshop where the clock had been stuck on 3:47 for sixteen hours, the owner looked up from his newspaper and said to no one:*

*"Here we go."*

## Notes on this draft

This is a **sketch**—establishing tone, voice, the four perspectives, the wrongness made physical. Not final. Questions to resolve:

1. **The boy with the Magician** - who is he? A relative? Someone he's protecting? Remove him?

2. **The Priestess section** - does the photography angle work? Should she have different tools for tracking fractures?

3. **The Emperor section** - the mirror/shadow imagery might be too on-the-nose. Alternatives?

4. **The Empress section** - is the voice of the feeding clear without being explicit? Does the "something screamed" work or is it too much?

5. **The coda** - the pawnshop owner who comments. Is this too cute? Remove? Or develop him as a minor character who sees things?

6. **Length** - this is short. Opening could expand each section, or stay compressed and let the story breathe after.

7. **Tarot** - Death/Hanged Man/Tower are thematically present but not named. Should they be explicit? Epigraphs? Mentioned?
