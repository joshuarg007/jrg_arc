# Chapter 2: The Descent

---

## I.

The projects smelled like ammonia and despair.

The Magician had grown up here—not by birth, but by exile. Sent down from the clean house with the indifferent stepfather and the mother who'd chosen wrong. Sent into his father's domain, where the rules were different and the violence was honest.

He'd learned things in these hallways. How to spot a tweaker before they spotted you. How to walk without making sound. How to take a punch, and when to throw one, and when to run. By the time he was fourteen, he had a reputation. By fifteen, he'd stopped being prey.

Now he was back.

The boy walked beside him—the same boy from the car, the one with the too-old eyes. His name was Marcus, and he was the Magician's nephew, son of a cousin who'd overdosed three years ago. The Magician hadn't known about him until last month. Finding out he existed had felt like finding a crack in a wall he thought was solid.

"Why are we here?" Marcus asked.

"I need to talk to my father."

"He's not gonna want to talk."

"I know."

They climbed the stairs. Third floor. The elevator had been broken since the Magician was a kid, and he suspected it had been broken since the building went up. Fluorescent lights buzzed and flickered, making the hallway strobe. In one of the doorways, someone was slumped against the frame, breathing but not conscious.

The Magician stepped over them.

His father's apartment was at the end of the hall. Same door as always. Same smell seeping out from underneath—cigarette smoke and something sweeter, something that burned.

He knocked.

Nothing.

He knocked again.

"Fuck off," someone called from inside.

"It's me."

Silence. Then shuffling. Then the door cracked open, and a bloodshot eye appeared in the gap.

"Thought you were dead," his father said.

"Not yet."

"Pity."

The door opened wider.

---

## II.

The apartment was a museum of failure.

Same furniture from when the Magician was a kid, but more broken now. Holes in the drywall. Stains on the carpet that could have been anything. The TV was on—some infomercial about knives—and the volume was too loud, filling the space with manufactured enthusiasm.

His father sat in the recliner like it was a throne. Skinnier than he remembered. Older. The same violent energy contained in a frame that could barely contain itself anymore.

"So," his father said. "You need something. Otherwise you wouldn't be here."

"I need to know about the thing you served."

The TV went silent. Not muted—silent. The Magician wasn't sure which of them had done it.

"The fuck you talking about."

"Before you fell. Before you ended up here. You were training for something. With something. I need to know what it was."

His father's hands gripped the armrests. The knuckles went white.

"Who told you about that."

"Nobody. I figured it out. The way you know things you shouldn't know. The way time goes wrong around you. The way Mom said you used to be different, before." He leaned forward. "You touched the wrongness. You let it in. Then you ran away."

"I didn't run. I was thrown out."

"Same difference. What did you learn before they threw you?"

"Nothing that helps."

"Let me be the judge of that."

His father stared at him. The bloodshot eyes had something behind them now—something that might have been memory, or fear, or the echo of something vast and terrible.

"They're called the Witnesses," he said finally. "The order I was with. They serve what's behind the veil. Not to let it through—to... understand it. To learn its shapes. They think if they understand it well enough, they can use it."

"Can they?"

"Fuck no. Nobody uses that thing. It uses you. That's what I learned. That's why I ran." He laughed, harsh and broken. "Didn't run fast enough. It's still in me. Not controlling—just watching. Everything I do, it watches. Everything I feel, it feels. I'm a window for it. Have been for thirty years."

The Magician felt cold. "It's watching now?"

"Always watching." His father's eyes met his—and for a moment, just a moment, something else looked through them. Something curious. Something patient. Something that had been waiting a long time for this conversation.

Then it was gone, and his father slumped in the chair like a puppet with cut strings.

"Get out," he said. "You want to know about the wrongness, go find the Witnesses yourself. Tower off Champa Street. They'll let you in—they let everyone in. Getting out is the hard part."

"The Priestess mentioned fractures. Cracks in the city. Are they—"

"The Witnesses made them. Or widened them. Or something. They've been prying at the veil for decades. Trying to make the window bigger." He laughed again. "Guess they finally succeeded. Time's been fucked up for months now. Everyone thinks they're going crazy. They're not. They're just noticing."

The Magician stood. Marcus stood with him, silent the whole time, watching everything.

"One more thing," the Magician said. "The Emperor. You know that term?"

His father flinched. Actually flinched.

"Where did you hear that name."

"Someone used it. About someone I met."

"The vessels." His father's voice dropped to a whisper. "They bred them, centuries ago. Containers for the wrongness. Living prisons. The idea was if you put enough of it into one bloodline, kept it locked in their bones, it couldn't spread. Couldn't infect everyone." He shook his head. "Stupid fucking plan. All it did was concentrate the poison. Make it stronger. Every generation, the vessels get closer to breaking."

"What happens when they break?"

"What do you think happens? Everything the wrongness has been storing for five hundred years comes out at once. That's what the Witnesses are waiting for. That's their whole plan. Find the current vessel, break them open, ride the wave of chaos through to the other side."

The Magician thought about the man in Cheesman Park. The shaking. The dead grass. The thing pressing against the walls of his containment.

"I have to go."

"Of course you do. Hero shit." His father didn't get up. "Word of advice? Don't trust the prophecy. The four who are one—that's what they call you, right? The convergence? It's not a solution. It's a weapon. Someone designed that prophecy a long time ago, and it wasn't for your benefit."

"Then whose?"

His father smiled. There was no humor in it.

"That's the question, isn't it."

---

## III.

The Priestess found the tower off Champa Street at midnight.

It looked like an office building. Glass and steel, twelve stories, nothing to distinguish it from any other corporate shell in the city. But she could see what was underneath—the fractures spiraling up its face like cracks in ice, the wrongness pooling at its foundation, the way reality itself seemed to bend toward it like iron filings toward a magnet.

She'd been tracking these cracks for months. She'd never found their source.

Now she had.

The lobby was empty. Clean tile, neutral art, an unmanned security desk. The elevator dinged as she approached, doors sliding open as if expecting her.

*Invitation*, she thought. *They want me here.*

She stepped inside.

The button panel had twelve floors, but she'd seen the building from outside—it was twelve stories, nothing more. So when her finger pressed the button for the thirteenth floor, she wasn't surprised when the elevator accepted it.

Going up.

---

## IV.

The thirteenth floor was a library.

Not the clean corporate space she'd expected. Something older. Wooden shelves stretching to a ceiling she couldn't see. Candles instead of electric lights. The smell of paper and dust and something else—something sweet, something rotten, something that made her eyes water.

"You came faster than we expected."

She turned. A woman stood behind her—hadn't been there a moment ago—wearing robes that looked medieval and a smile that looked hungry.

"You're the Witnesses," the Priestess said.

"One of them. I'm called the Reader. I archive the visions. Record the prophecies. Keep track of what the wrongness shows us." The Reader tilted her head. "You have a gift. Sight beyond sight. We've been watching you. Hoping you'd find your way here."

"I'm not here to join you."

"Of course not. You're here to understand. To learn about the cracks you've been mapping. To find out what's really happening to the veil." The Reader gestured toward a reading table, where a book lay open. "Please. Let me show you."

The Priestess hesitated. Everything about this was wrong—the invitation, the library, the woman's hungry smile. But she hadn't spent months mapping fractures to turn away at the threshold.

She approached the table.

The book was old. Older than printing. Handwritten in a language she didn't recognize but somehow understood.

*The Seal and the Membrane*, the title read. *A History of the Veil.*

"The veil was never meant to be a wall," the Reader said, standing beside her. "That's the great misunderstanding. The great tragedy. Millennia ago, it was permeable. Things passed through. Not monsters—ideas. Inspirations. Dreams. The wrongness isn't wrong, you see. It's just different. Another way of being. And it wanted to know us the way we wanted to know it."

"What happened?"

"Fear. As always. Someone decided the exchange was too dangerous. Someone decided we needed to be protected from ourselves." The Reader turned a page. "They built the seal. Hardened the membrane into a barrier. Locked us in and the other out."

The Priestess looked at the illustrations—diagrams of the veil, before and after. The membrane version looked alive, breathing, dynamic. The seal version looked dead. A scar across reality.

"That's what's breaking," she said. "Not the veil. The seal."

"Yes. The original membrane is trying to reassert itself. The cracks aren't damage—they're healing. The wrongness leaking through isn't invasion—it's reconnection." The Reader smiled. "We've been trying to help it along. Widening the cracks. Easing the pressure. Making space for what was always meant to happen."

"The temporal distortions—"

"Side effects. The seal was never meant to last this long. Time itself is confused about which version of reality is correct. It should settle, once the membrane is fully restored."

The Priestess felt her worldview shifting. Everything she'd assumed—that the cracks were bad, that the wrongness was wrong, that healing meant closing—turned inside out.

"Why are you telling me this?"

"Because you're part of the prophecy. One of the four. And the prophecy isn't what you think it is." The Reader turned another page. "The four who are one—you, the Magician, the Emperor, the Empress—you were designed. Bred. Guided toward each other across generations. Not to save anything. To catalyze the final transformation."

"Designed by who?"

"By us. By the Witnesses. Centuries ago." The Reader's smile widened. "The prophecy isn't a warning. It's a recipe. And you're the ingredients."

The Priestess stepped back from the table.

"I don't believe you."

"You don't have to. The convergence will happen regardless. The four will meet. The seal will break. The membrane will restore. And whatever comes through..." The Reader shrugged. "We'll see. That's rather the point, isn't it? To finally see."

The candles flickered. The Priestess felt the wrongness pressing against the library's walls, eager and patient and vast.

She ran.

---

## V.

The Emperor found him first.

Not in Cheesman Park—that came later. In a bar off Larimer Street, three days after the Priestess escaped the tower, two days after the Magician learned about the vessels.

He knew what the man was immediately. There's a way people carry themselves when they're holding something terrible inside—careful, controlled, always one bad moment from catastrophe. The Emperor had it written in every movement.

"You're one of the four," the Emperor said without preamble.

"So I'm told."

"The Witnesses are hunting us. All of us. They want to force the convergence."

"I know. I visited my father. He told me."

"Your father." The Emperor's eyes narrowed. "He was one of them. The fallen priest."

"He told you that?"

"No. But I've spent my whole life learning about the people who made me. The order that bred my bloodline. The Witnesses created the vessels. Created me." He sat down heavily. "They think if the four converge at the right moment, when the seal is weakest, we can crack it open completely. Let everything through at once."

"That sounds bad."

"It's apocalyptic. The wrongness has been building pressure behind the seal for millennia. If it breaks all at once—" He shook his head. "I've felt what's inside me. Just the fragment they put in my ancestors. The full thing would unmake everything."

"Then we don't let them force the convergence."

"It might not matter. The Priestess—the woman who sees the cracks—she discovered something at the tower. The seal is already failing. The convergence might happen naturally, without anyone forcing it. Four points of pressure, four cracks in the seal, four pieces of a key that's been scattered for centuries."

The Magician thought about the Empress. About his brother. About futures being eaten.

"There's a fourth person," he said. "A woman. I think she's the Empress—the one who creates. But something's been feeding on her. Draining her power."

"The puppet." The Emperor nodded grimly. "We know about him. The Witnesses use people like that. Parasites to keep the pieces separated until they're ready. Your brother isn't acting alone."

"I figured that out."

"Can you free her?"

"I don't know. I don't even know where she is. We knew each other as kids, but that was—"

"Downing Street. She's in an apartment on Downing Street." The Emperor pulled out a photograph—surveillance, looked like, taken from across the street. The woman in the window. The shape the Magician had recognized.

"How do you have this?"

"I've been watching. All of us. Trying to understand the board before the game started." The Emperor slid the photo across the bar. "The puppet is with her most of the time. But he leaves. Every night, around 2 AM, he goes to the tower to report. You'd have a few hours."

"To do what? I can't just walk in and—"

"You can." The Emperor leaned forward. "You're stronger than you know. The Magician's gift is transformation—changing what is into what could be. You've been using it instinctively all your life. The fights you shouldn't have won. The escapes you shouldn't have made. Your grandfather knew what you were. He was training you to use it consciously."

"He died before he could finish."

"Then finish yourself. You have the tools. You just need to use them." The Emperor stood. "I'll be in Cheesman Park. When you're ready—when she's free—bring her there. The Priestess is already watching, mapping the fractures for when we need to move."

"And then what?"

"Then we figure out if the Witnesses are right. If the convergence really is a weapon. And if it is—" The Emperor's face hardened. "We figure out how to aim it."

He left.

The Magician sat alone in the bar, holding a photograph of a woman he used to protect, thinking about transformation and weapons and the shape of what was coming.

The bartender came over.

"Another drink?"

The Magician looked at him. Really looked. The man's eyes were wrong—too steady, too knowing, watching with an attention that didn't belong to a bartender in a dive bar.

"You're one of them," the Magician said. "The Witnesses."

The bartender smiled.

"We're everywhere. Watching. Waiting. The convergence is coming whether you want it or not. The question is whether you'll be instruments or obstacles." He wiped a glass with a rag. "I'd recommend instrument. The obstacles don't tend to last long."

The Magician stood.

"Tell your order something for me."

"What's that?"

"I'm not an instrument. I'm not an obstacle. I'm the thing that comes for people who try to use me as either."

He walked out into the night.

Behind him, the bartender laughed.

"We're counting on it," he said to the empty bar.

---

## VI.

She woke up alone.

The Empress—though she didn't know that was her name yet—reached for him in the dark and found nothing. Empty sheets. Cold pillow. The space where he should have been, vacant.

Relief flooded through her before she could stop it.

*He's gone. He's gone. I can think. I can—*

Then guilt. Crushing, manufactured guilt. How could she feel relieved? She loved him. She loved him. She—

*Stop.*

The voice came from inside. From the place she'd buried. From the part of herself she'd locked away at eight years old and never visited again.

*This isn't love. This is what he made you feel. It's not real. Stop.*

She sat up in bed. The room looked different without him in it. Smaller. Clearer. The shadows didn't press as hard.

*You know what he is. You've always known. You just couldn't admit it because admitting it meant admitting what he'd done. What he's been doing.*

She thought about the exhaustion that never lifted. The lost hours. The sense that she used to be more and now was less.

*He's eating you. He's been eating you for fifteen years. And you let him because you were afraid.*

"I couldn't stop him," she whispered to the empty room.

*You could. You just forgot how.*

She closed her eyes. Reached inside, into the locked place, the buried place, the space where something used to live before she killed it.

And felt it stir.

Not dead. Sleeping.

Waiting.

*Make something*, the voice said. *Make something small. Remember what it felt like.*

She didn't know if she could. Didn't know if there was anything left after so many years of draining. But she tried.

She thought about light. About warmth. About the spark that creates instead of consumes.

Her palm tingled.

She opened her eyes.

In her hand, glowing softly, was a single mote of light. The size of a firefly. Warm. Real. Hers.

"Oh," she breathed. "Oh."

Tears ran down her face. She didn't know if they were grief or joy. Both, maybe. The light flickered, fragile, barely there—but there. Still there. After everything.

The door opened.

He was home.

She closed her fist. The light vanished. The trained response kicked in—smile, soften, become what he wanted—but something was different this time. Something underneath. A coal that was still burning.

He crossed to the bed. Kissed her forehead. Wrapped around her the way he always did.

"Missed me?" he said.

"Always," she said.

But inside, in the place where the coal burned, she was already planning.

*Not yet*, the voice said. *Not yet. But soon.*

## VII.

*The Moon. Card XVIII.*

*Illusion. Fear. The unconscious. The path between two towers, where nothing is what it seems and even the light lies.*

*The dog and the wolf howl at reflected light. The crayfish emerges from the water—something rising from the depths, something that doesn't want to be seen.*

*The Moon doesn't create darkness. It illuminates what the darkness contains. And sometimes, that's worse.*

*Sometimes, seeing clearly is the most terrifying thing of all.*
